Everybody is doing that like at least multiple times a day.
We quickly click "Accept All" to pass the pop-up that annoyingly stays between us and the article that we want to read. 

I used to often ask myself: What happens behind the scenes when you click these buttons? And more importantly for those who manage businesses, what happens when you click the "decline" button?

For quite some time, I've been observing companies panicking over this daily here in France, where I'm actually operating. 
Businesses here are getting deeply terrified of losing their valuable analytics data because of strict European privacy laws that are getting even stricter. 

Now that I have found the answers, I will try to explain the truth about how website tracking works today and how it is quietly changing. 

The Old Way: The Megaphone
That's how most websites have been built for the last ten years, by using a "public megaphone" approach. When a user enters your website and once they accept the cookies, the megaphone stays on, the website's code would shout to Google or Facebook, saying: "This person just scrolled down to the page and clicked a button, interested in our shoes." 
But nowadays because of privacy concerns, millions of people have adblockers installed on their browsers and choose to click "Decline Cookies" 
The data dies immediately when that happens. This is sometimes equal to a business losing up to 30% of its actual data.

The New Way: The Hidden-Private Sorting Room
I rebuilt a tracking system for a project recently to move away from the "megaphone" and built a Server-Side Pipeline. You may want to think of it like a private sorting room.
Now the website whispers to the company's own private server instead of shouting to Google or Meta. Here is why this changes everything:

- It increases the speed of the website: Heavy, third-party tracking codes from the website itself are gone. And naturally the website will see performance increases; this is especially essential for your landing page optimization (e.g. if you're running Google Ads campaigns)

- Respects privacy laws and privacy: The server acts like a bouncer. If a user declines the cookies, it instantly deletes their data before it ever reaches tech giants like Google.

- The data becomes more accurate: Adblockers usually ignore a company's own private server. This allows businesses to safely track anonymous, big-picture trends (like total page views) without compromising user privacy.

The Summary
The era of copy-pasting tracking codes and hoping for magic is over. Upgrading your plumbing is the only solution right now to respect European privacy laws and get accurate business data.
